Data art and representation

This essay will conduct a close reading of various artworks and determine their relationship between data art and data visualisation, and their strategies of representation. According to Kirell Benzi (2020), data art is a data set which is conveyed through artist practises to elicit emotions from the audience. This can take the form of music, interactive pieces, images, etc. Because data art is art and it can thus be understood differently based on a viewer’s context.

Sabine Roeser in the article How Art Can Contribute to Ethical Reflection on Risky Technologies, suggests that techno-art (ie. art which reflects on and engages with risky technology) can be used to enhance understanding and broaden a person’s perspectives. As such, she believes that this will lead to better technological innovations because they will be made with more compassion and kindness.

Within explorations of techno-art, various artists have produced works which primarily focus on either the embracing of technologies (as enhancements for people, or as objects which create a more efficient society) or the warning of rapid technological development (shown in works where robots take over the world or humans become addicted to technology). Roeser explains that works such as these, provide audiences with creative perspectives on topics and reflections on issues within society, however, she argues that these are the necessary aspects which provide people with deeper moral insight. One of the most recent examples, I think, of something which questions morals within the creation of technology is the claims of sentience of a Google AI programme (Maruf, 2022). AI programmes which are able to “learn” by recognising their errors and changing their behaviour, could be argued to have some human characteristics, however, the boundaries blur more when programs are able to be “creative” or “perceive themselves”. This brings into question the ethics of testing on a sentient being, and how its creators should care for it, but it is also a reminder of the dangers of this technology. However, I feel that these programs are the representation of algorithms who take data and reflect learned qualities which could be perceived as human. Thus, when we represent data in ways which feel innately human, we reflect on our moral philosophies (Roeser, 2016).

Fernanda B. Viégas and Martin Wattenberg (2007), however, believe that messages are embodied within the data art itself. Unlike Roeser who used examples of techno-art, Viégas and Wattenberg believe that data art should be based on the data itself. The artwork thus produces a new perspective of the data. Viégas and Wattenberg present a similar data-artwork as the figure below which takes the average colours of the frames within a movie, and places the in one image as long strips. Within the Harry Potter films, we can see from this strip all the moments of darkness and the sudden moments of light within it – a pattern which is reminiscent of the mantra “out of darkness comes light”. I believe that this is what Viégas and Wattenberg (2007) were saying about the way in which data-art can reveal messages which might not always be obvious just by analysing the data alone. There should be some brainwork in which the viewer must apply their own context and thoughts into the decoding of the artwork.

One of the prevalent concepts which is frequently mentioned throughout both of the previous articles, is this constructionist idea (Hall, 1997) that meaning is created and not inherently found within artefacts (objects) and signs (languages, images, etc). The decoding of these signs in order to understand its message is affected by one’s own personal biases and context. However, one must also consider the history (which would reflect biases of the time period), presentation, and creation of the artefact (Kidd, 2015). All of these factors contribute into the intended meaning by the creator in comparison to the meaning which one might decode from it. Because meaning is created, artefacts play a large role in both how people identify themselves, but also how they identify others. Historically, the people in power were the primary creators of this identity relationship. However, this meant that people who did not have an equal amount of power at the time, were not able to contribute to the representation of themselves. Understanding the effect of one’s own biases on the identity of the Other, and acting to compensate for it, will allow us to create technologies which are more presentative of a broader spectrum of people. When applying this logic to the creation of technology, one could understand how the Facebook AI mistakenly recognised people of colour as primates. Facebook lacked the implementation of people of colour when considering the creation of their face recognition AI. This led to scandal, and offense from members of the community (BBC News, 2021).

In conclusion, data-art and techno-art are both art forms which are laden with messages which need to be decoded by the viewer. Whilst these art forms can broaden a viewer’s perspective, or provide new ideas for them to explore, the creator must be considerate of not only their own biases when creating an artwork, but additionally the effects of the messages within their work on others. When this is considered, as well as the effects on the viewers, one can create kinder technology which is more inclusive.
