Summary of What is Interaction for Data Visualization

Research methods

In the article What is Interaction for Data Visualization by Evanthia Dimara and Charles Perin (2020), the concept of interaction within data visualisation is explored and studied. The gathers data and forms information in various ways. The first is through a survey in which they asked visualisation experts and researchers for: papers on the definition of interaction in data visualisation, relevant papers on interaction as a broader topic, the number of years they have in visualisation, and to rate their interaction experience, describing how often their own papers focus on interaction. This survey included definition research papers, and these papers were included if they had sufficient impact and relevance (which the researchers calculated through a paper’s forward and backward references). The researchers then catagorised the data into tags which described the nature of the paper’s which were included and suggested (eg. Complaint paper, definitional paper, interactive pipeline, etc).

Definitional research papers

The papers suggested in the survey by researchers show that interaction for visualisation must involve external entities such as the user and the data, external action-reactions between the user and the visualisation system, internal actions from the user through cognitive actions (reasoning and analytics), and internal entities such as intent and time. However, interaction in visualisation is not limited to the aforementioned things. It can also involve external entities such as a mouse, keyboard, etc; internal entities such as prior knowledge, skills, etc; external actions such as filtering tools which change the data and provide new representations; internal actions such as memory encoding, simulative reasoning, etc. Interaction, which is a goal focused activity that contains sequential, incremental and iterative semantics, has properties of granularity, discrete and continuous interactions, and direct and indirect interactions.

Benefits and critiques papers

Interaction in visualisation is positively seen as a means to amplify understanding of data and enhance exploration of said data. Furthermore, it is a means for users to remain in control of how they experience the data by leveraging natural abilities, thus becoming more accessible to broader audiences. However, interaction is rarely the focus when designing visualisations, and visualisation systems are inflexible. Additionally, it both overfocuses and fails to focus on user goals. The downfalls of interaction in visualisation seems to be due to technological challenges, costs, limited modalities and more.

Interaction concept papers

These results show the types of approaches there are to creating interaction in visualisations. A system-centric approach focuses on the system operations. This approach has specific domains, and an organised network which is in a hierarchy. However, this approach does not specify the role of the user or how they interact. The task-centric approach describes the low-level tasks of interaction which are data types such as roles, means, targets, etc. and whilst low high-level visualization tasks build on low-level interactions, the boundaries between low, intermediate and high-level interactions are not defined. A human-centric approach thinks about interaction in terms of user-intent. This approach contains high-level cognitive activities such as insight, meta and provenance actions, but assumes a high-level of users intent which can be considered within a specific context. An approach like this can become very broad and thus lead to more questions of how it can be used to accommodate wider spectrums of user intents.

Visualisation pipeline papers

The papers from this section stressed that interaction could affect all levels of the visualisation pipeline. However, they each had different focuses. Some papers focused on categorisations of transformations according to which level they affect. Other focused on interaction techniques and others on categorisations of interaction. The visualisation pipeline framework is system-centric and therefore does not include user intent. Furthermore, it is not flexible enough to describe which interactions are available to a user.

Visualisation and HCI

Visualisation and HCI are two views of interaction design. Within visualisation, interaction is seen as a dialogue between the user and the system about the data. HCI also has dialogue, which is channelled through input and output from a computer. This dialogue should provide a strong sense of control (through the use of tools) and understanding for users – both of which should minimise errors and maximise the user’s capabilities. Furthermore, the dialogue should be an error-free transmission of data. HCI further states that the technology should feel natural for the user to use and should provide them with a satisfying experience which meets their expectations. By creating user profiling, designers may be able to create more tasks which are supported and make an overall more inclusive experience. Visualisation and HCI have some key points of difference. For starters, visualisation considers data within its interactions; HCI has a performance focus, whereas visualisation has a user engagement and analysis focus; visualisation believes intent evolves whereas HCI believes that intent is formed externally; visualisation builds understanding whilst HCI wants users to interact with all levels of the pipeline.

Thoughts

Good practices of interaction in visualisation are similar to the practise of good user interface. Like in visualisation, users should be able to intuitively use your interface, and when they interact with the interface, it should provide results which are to a user’s expectations. Whilst the context in which these are applied might differ; I feel that the concept remains the same: ultimately a designer is creating their interface to be satisfying for the user, and easily accessible for broad audiences.
